## 2. Major Thought Turning Points

### Turning Point 1: Childhood Contact with Philosophy (~133 AD)

**Age: ~12 years old**

Marcus Aurelius's earliest and most formative turning point was his encounter with Stoic philosophy as a child. Around age 11-12, he began adopting the dress and habits of a philosopher:

- He slept on the ground (his mother had to persuade him to use a bed)
- He wore a rough Greek cloak (*tribon*), the mark of a Cynic or Stoic philosopher
- He devoted himself to intensive study under multiple philosophical tutors

**Key Teachers (from *Meditations* Book 1):**

| Teacher | Subject | Influence |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Junius Rusticus** | Stoic philosophy | Introduced Marcus to Epictetus's *Discourses*; taught him moral integrity. Marcus credits him for learning to live a philosophical life. |
| **Apollonius of Chalcedon** | Stoic philosophy | Taught moral rigor and intellectual discipline. Marcus praised his unwavering commitment to principle. |
| **Sextus of Chaeronea** | Ethics (Platonic/Stoic) | Grandson of Plutarch. Taught ethics and the examined life. Marcus learned kindness and the importance of family. |
| **Alexander the Platonist** | Greek philosophy | Broadened Marcus's philosophical education beyond strict Stoicism. |
| **Marcus Cornelius Fronto** | Latin rhetoric | Their surviving correspondence reveals a warm personal relationship. Fronto taught literary style and oratory. |
| **Herodes Atticus** | Greek rhetoric | Taught Greek rhetoric and literature. Marcus became fluent in Greek. |

**Philosophical Significance:**
- This early immersion established the Stoic framework that would guide Marcus for the rest of his life
- The emphasis on **virtue, duty, self-discipline, and acceptance of fate** became the foundation of *Meditations*
- Marcus's gratitude to these teachers (expressed in Book 1) shows how deeply this formation shaped his identity

> Source: *Meditations* Book 1 (primary source), *Historia Augusta* (Vita Marci). **Very high credibility** -- Marcus's own testimony.

### Turning Point 2: Adoption by Antoninus Pius (138 AD)

**Age: 17 years old**

The adoption orchestrated by Emperor Hadrian in February 138 AD was the pivotal moment that set Marcus on the path to becoming emperor. This event transformed him from a philosophical student into an heir-apparent with enormous political responsibilities.

**Key aspects:**
- Hadrian's succession plan was innovative: he chose Antoninus Pius (a capable administrator) as a transitional figure, with Marcus and Lucius Verus as the long-term heirs
- Marcus received the title **Caesar** and began his political career
- He had to balance his philosophical inclinations with the demands of imperial administration
- For 23 years (138-161), Marcus served under Antoninus Pius, learning governance

**Philosophical Significance:**
- This was when **duty** (*officium*) became central to Marcus's philosophy
- The tension between philosophical contemplation and political responsibility became a recurring theme in *Meditations*
- Marcus's praise of Antoninus in Book 1 reveals how deeply he internalized the model of a just, patient, and dutiful ruler

> Source: *Historia Augusta*, Cassius Dio, *Meditations* Book 1. **Very high credibility**.

---

### Turning Point 3: Becoming Emperor (161 AD)

**Age: 39 years old**

The death of Antoninus Pius on March 7, 161 AD and Marcus's accession to the throne marked the transition from apprentice to ruler. This was the moment when the philosophical ideals Marcus had cultivated for decades were put to the ultimate test.

**Key aspects:**
- Marcus immediately elevated Lucius Verus to co-emperor -- an act of generosity and political wisdom
- The Parthian War began almost immediately, testing his military leadership
- The Antonine Plague struck, creating unprecedented humanitarian and economic crises
- Marcus had to make life-and-death decisions affecting millions

**Philosophical Significance:**
- The *Meditations* reflect the immense pressure of imperial power: "At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: 'I have to go to work -- as a human being.'" (Book 5.1)
- Marcus constantly reminded himself that power, fame, and even life itself are transient
- His Stoic practice became more urgent and personal as he faced the realities of war, plague, and betrayal

> Source: Cassius Dio, *Historia Augusta*, *Meditations*. **Very high credibility**.

---

### Turning Point 4: The Marcomannic Wars (166-180 AD)

**Age: 45-59 years old**

The Marcomannic Wars were the defining experience of Marcus's reign and the crucible in which *Meditations* was composed. These wars transformed him from a philosopher-emperor in Rome to a philosopher-general on the frontier.

**Key aspects:**
- Germanic tribes broke through the Danube frontier, threatening Italy itself
- Marcus spent the last 14 years of his life largely on campaign
- The Antonine Plague devastated the army and the population
- Marcus was forced to recruit slaves, gladiators, and barbarians into the legions
- He wrote *Meditations* in military tents, far from the comforts of Rome

**Philosophical Significance:**
- The austere conditions of military life stripped away all luxury and reminded Marcus of what truly matters
- The constant proximity to death deepened his reflections on mortality and impermanence
- The "Thundering Legion" incident (172 AD) forced him to confront questions of divine providence and fate
- The experience of leading men in battle reinforced his Stoic emphasis on duty, courage, and rational action

> Source: Cassius Dio, *Historia Augusta*, scholarly analysis. **Very high credibility**.

---

### Turning Point 5: The Avidius Cassius Revolt (175 AD)

**Age: 54 years old**

The revolt of Avidius Cassius was a profound personal and philosophical crisis for Marcus. A trusted general and hero of the Parthian War declared himself emperor, reportedly after receiving false reports of Marcus's death.

**Key aspects:**
- Cassius was a capable military commander who had served Marcus well
- The revolt lasted only ~3 months before Cassius was assassinated by his own men
- Some sources suggest Faustina may have been involved
- Marcus's response was remarkable: he showed clemency and burned Cassius's correspondence

**Philosophical Significance:**
- Marcus's response to betrayal was a direct application of Stoic principles: "The best revenge is not to be like your enemy" (Book 8.38)
- His decision to burn the correspondence showed his commitment to justice over vengeance
- The death of Faustina during the eastern journey added personal loss to political crisis
- This event reinforced Marcus's belief in the Stoic ideal of the philosopher-ruler who responds to adversity with reason and compassion

> Source: Cassius Dio, *Historia Augusta*. **High credibility**.

## 3. Writing Timeline of Meditations

### Overview

- **Original title:** Τὰ εἰς ἑαυτόν (*Ta eis heauton*) -- "Things to Himself"
- **Language:** Koine Greek (not Latin, despite Marcus being a Roman emperor)
- **Writing period:** ~170-180 AD (over approximately 10 years)
- **Location:** Military camps along the Danube frontier during the Marcomannic Wars
- **Intent:** Private philosophical journal, never intended for publication
- **Structure:** 12 books (likely corresponding to 12 physical notebooks or codices)

### Scholarly Dating by Books

The following dating is based on the work of scholars including **A.S.L. Farquharson** (1944), **C.R. Haines** (Loeb edition), **Pierre Hadot** (*The Inner Citadel*), and **Anthony Birley** (*Marcus Aurelius: A Biography*).

| Book | Estimated Date | Likely Location | Context |
|------|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Book 1** | Possibly ~170 AD or earlier | Possibly at **Aquincum** (modern Budapest) or Rome | Stands apart as a list of **debts of gratitude** to teachers, family, and friends. May have been written as a preface or at a different time from the rest. |
| **Book 2** | ~170 AD | **Carnuntum** (modern Austria, near Petronell-Carnuntum) | Written "among the Quadi" (as noted in the text). Reflects on mortality and the brevity of life. |
| **Book 3** | ~170-171 AD | **Carnuntum** or nearby frontier camp | Focuses on inner purpose and self-discipline. Written during the early Marcomannic campaigns. |
| **Book 4** | ~171 AD | **Carnuntum** or frontier region | One of the most quoted books. Themes of withdrawal, simplicity, and acceptance. |
| **Book 5** | ~171 AD | Frontier military camp | Famous opening about struggling to get out of bed. Themes of duty and resilience. |
| **Book 6** | ~172 AD | Between **Carnuntum and Aquincum**, or at various campaign stations | Focuses on tolerance and self-mastery. |
| **Book 7** | ~172-173 AD | Danube campaign region | Reflects on transience, fame, and inner strength. |
| **Book 8** | ~173-174 AD | Danube campaign region | Themes of self-alignment and purpose. |
| **Book 9** | ~174-175 AD | Danube campaign region | Deals with justice, virtue, and cosmic unity. Written during the period of the Avidius Cassius revolt. |
| **Book 10** | ~176-177 AD | Possibly **Sirmium** (modern Sremska Mitrovica, Serbia) or other headquarters | Reaffirms commitment to Stoic philosophy. Written after the Cassius revolt and Faustina's death. |
| **Book 11** | ~177-178 AD | Campaign headquarters | Explores character, social relations, and self-examination. |
| **Book 12** | ~178-180 AD | **Vindobona** (modern Vienna) or final campaign headquarters | The final book -- almost a valedictory address. Reflects on life's end and legacy. |

### Important Notes on the Writing Timeline

1. **Not arranged chronologically:** The books are not in chronological order. They represent a personal journal, not a planned treatise.
2. **Physical notebooks:** The chapter/book numbering likely represents physical notebooks rather than sequential writing periods.
3. **Internal evidence:** Scholars date the books based on:
   - References to places (e.g., Book 2 "among the Quadi")
   - References to age and health
   - Stylistic analysis and thematic development
   - Historical events referenced
4. **Two main phases:** Most scholars identify two main phases of composition:
   - **Phase 1 (Books 1-6):** ~170-172 AD, early campaigns
   - **Phase 2 (Books 7-12):** ~172-180 AD, later campaigns
5. **Tone shift:** Some scholars note a shift in tone between the two halves, with the later books reflecting deeper Stoic acceptance and a more melancholy awareness of mortality.

> Source: Farquharson (1944), Haines (Loeb), Hadot (*The Inner Citadel*), Birley (*Marcus Aurelius: A Biography*). **Medium-high credibility** -- scholarly consensus based on internal evidence, but exact dates remain conjecture.

## 4. Book-by-Book Themes

### Book 1: Gratitude and Influences
- **Core theme:** Recognizing and honoring those who helped form your virtue
- Marcus lists debts of gratitude to: his grandfather (good morals), his mother (piety, simplicity), his great-grandfather (not attending public schools), Hadrian ("Verissimus"), his philosophical tutors, and Antoninus Pius (patience, justice, simplicity)
- **Key idea:** Character is shaped by relationships and example

### Book 2: Mortality and Perspective
- **Core theme:** Life is short; don't waste it on trivialities
- Written "among the Quadi" on the Danube frontier
- **Key quote:** "Think of yourself as dead. You have lived your life. Now, take what's left and live it properly."
- Warns against being controlled by anger or external circumstances

### Book 3: Inner Purpose and Self-Discipline
- **Core theme:** Aligning actions with inner purpose
- "Withdraw into yourself; focus on reason and justice"
- Happiness comes from doing one's duty well
- Stresses the brevity of life and the importance of living with intention

### Book 4: Withdrawal, Simplicity, and Acceptance
- **Core theme:** Happiness lies within; external things cannot harm the soul
- **Key quote:** "The universe is change; our life is what our thoughts make it"
- Encourages retreat into one's own mind for peace
- Discusses accepting one's role in the cosmic order
- One of the most frequently quoted books

### Book 5: Duty, Resilience, and Morning Resolve
- **Core theme:** We were born for cooperation; do your work with diligence
- Famous opening about struggling to get out of bed: "At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: 'I have to go to work -- as a human being.'"
- Encourages facing each day with purpose regardless of feelings
- Reinforces Stoic duty to society and rational nature

### Book 6: Tolerance, Self-Mastery, and Reason
- **Core theme:** How to respond to difficult people
- "Meet hatred with kindness; correct through teaching, not punishment"
- Anger harms the angry person more than the target
- Stresses the supremacy of reason over emotion

### Book 7: Transience, Fame, and Inner Strength
- **Core theme:** The impermanence of all things
- **Key quote:** "All things fade and become legend, then are swallowed by forgetfulness"
- Fame is fleeting; even the greatest are forgotten
- Encourages finding solace in nature and philosophical reflection

### Book 8: Self-Alignment and Purpose
- **Core theme:** Living consistently with one's values
- **Key quote:** "The best revenge is not to be like your enemy"
- Encourages asking: "Is this necessary?" before every action
- Stresses living in harmony with nature and accepting fate (*amor fati*)

### Book 9: Justice, Virtue, and Cosmic Unity
- **Core theme:** Wrongdoing and forgiveness
- A wrongdoer harms himself; do not add to it by retaliating
- Emphasizes the interconnectedness of all things in the universe
- Reflects on how all things come from and return to the universal whole

### Book 10: Harmony with Nature and Acceptance
- **Core theme:** Accepting what happens as part of nature's design
- Reflects on the goodness embedded in the natural order
- Encourages kindness, humility, and authenticity
- Reaffirms commitment to Stoic philosophy

### Book 11: Character, Social Relations, and Self-Examination
- **Core theme:** Relationships with others and self-knowledge
- "When offended, look inward -- have you done the same?"
- Discusses how our perceptions shape our experience
- Encourages seeing through appearances to the underlying reality

### Book 12: Life's End, Legacy, and Final Wisdom
- **Core theme:** Life is short; act with virtue now, not tomorrow
- Reflects on how those who sought fame are now forgotten
- Encourages embracing death without fear
- **Key quote:** "Think of the whole of existence, of which you are the tiniest part; think of the whole of time, of which a brief instant has been assigned to you"
- Almost a valedictory address

### Overarching Themes Across All 12 Books

| Theme | Greek/Latin Term | Description |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| Remember death | *Memento Mori* | Life is fleeting; live accordingly |
| Love your fate | *Amor Fati* | Accept what happens as part of nature's design |
| The inner citadel | (Hadot's term) | Your mind is your fortress; nothing external can harm your soul |
| Cosmic perspective | *Sympatheia* | You are a small part of an enormous whole |
| Duty and service | *Officium* | We are made for cooperation, like feet, hands, and eyes |
| Impermanence | *Panta rhei* | Everything changes; attachment to permanence causes suffering |
| Reason over emotion | *Logos* | Let the governing reason within you lead |
| Present focus | *Nun* | The past is gone, the future uncertain; only the present is yours |

> Source: Pierre Hadot (*The Inner Citadel*), various translations. **High credibility** -- scholarly consensus.

## 5. Historical Context: The Five Good Emperors

### Overview

The **Five Good Emperors** is a term coined by Niccolo Machiavelli and popularized by Edward Gibbon. They ruled the Roman Empire during its zenith, spanning approximately **96-180 AD**, largely overlapping with the **Pax Romana** (Roman Peace), a period of relative peace and stability lasting from 27 BC to 180 AD.

### The Five Emperors

| Emperor | Reign | Key Contributions | Relationship to Marcus |
|---------|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Nerva** | 96-98 AD | Established peaceful succession by adopting Trajan | Distant predecessor |
| **Trajan** | 98-117 AD | Expanded empire to greatest territorial extent; major building projects | Distant predecessor |
| **Hadrian** | 117-138 AD | Consolidated borders; built Hadrian's Wall; promoted Greek culture | Discovered and mentored Marcus |
| **Antoninus Pius** | 138-161 AD | Longest peaceful reign; administrative reforms; maintained stability | Adoptive father; ruled for 23 years while Marcus learned |
| **Marcus Aurelius** | 161-180 AD | Philosopher-emperor; *Meditations*; faced wars and plague | -- |

### Key Characteristics of the Era

- **Adoptive succession:** Each emperor chose a capable successor rather than relying on hereditary rule
- **Military strength** balanced with diplomatic governance
- **Legal reforms** and public works
- **Economic prosperity** and trade expansion
- Often considered the **high point of Roman civilization**
- The period ended with Marcus Aurelius, who broke the adoptive tradition by naming his biological son **Commodus** as successor

### Edward Gibbon's Assessment

Edward Gibbon famously wrote in *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* (1776):

> "If a man were called to fix the period in the history of the world, during which the condition of the human race was most happy and prosperous, he would, without hesitation, name that which elapsed from the death of Domitian to the accession of Commodus."

> Source: Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Chapter 1-2. Machiavelli, *Discourses on Livy*. **High credibility** -- standard historical interpretation.

## 6. The Antonine Plague (165-180+ AD)

### Overview

- **Also known as:** The Plague of Galen (named after the Greek physician who described it)
- **Type:** Likely **smallpox** (*variola*) or possibly **measles** -- exact pathogen is debated by modern scholars
- **Duration:** ~165-180 AD, with possible recurrences into the 3rd century
- **Origin:** Brought back to the Roman Empire by soldiers returning from the Parthian War (165-166 AD)

### Impact on the Roman Empire

| Impact Area | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Death toll** | Estimated **5-10 million** people; possibly killing up to **1/3 of the population** in some regions |
| **Military** | Severely weakened Roman legions, especially along the Danube frontier during the Marcomannic Wars |
| **Economy** | Labor shortages, agricultural decline, inflation |
| **Army** | Marcus was forced to recruit **slaves, gladiators, and Germanic barbarians** into the army to replenish losses |
| **Politics** | Contributed to succession concerns and the decision to name Commodus as heir |
| **Culture** | Boosted the spread of Christianity, as Christians were noted for caring for the sick |

### Significance for Marcus Aurelius

The plague profoundly tested Marcus's Stoic resolve and influenced the deeply reflective, often melancholy tone of the *Meditations*. His writings about **transience, death, and the impermanence of all things** were likely deepened by witnessing mass death.

> Source: Galen (primary physician source), Cassius Dio, Kyle Harper (*The Fate of Rome: Climate, Disease, and the End of an Empire*). **Very high credibility** -- well-documented by contemporary sources.

## 7. The Marcomannic Wars (166-180 AD)

### Overview

The Marcomannic Wars were a series of conflicts fought between the Roman Empire and various Germanic and Sarmatian tribes along the **Danube frontier**, primarily during the reign of Marcus Aurelius.

### Causes

- Pressure from migrating tribes, possibly pushed by other groups (including the Huns)
- Destabilization caused by the Antonine Plague
- Weakening of Roman frontier defenses
- Ambition of Germanic tribal leaders

### Major Tribes Involved

- **Marcomanni** (in modern Czech Republic/Bavaria)
- **Quadi** (in modern Slovakia/Moravia)
- **Iazyges** (Sarmatian, in modern Hungary)
- **Costoboci** (in modern Romania/Moldova)
- **Various other Germanic and Sarmatian tribes**

### Timeline of Major Events

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 166-167 AD | Tribes break through Danube defenses |
| 168-169 AD | Marcus and Lucius Verus campaign on the frontier |
| 169 AD | Lucius Verus dies; Marcus becomes sole emperor |
| 170 AD | **Siege of Aquileia** -- tribes penetrate into northern Italy |
| 170-175 AD | First Marcomannic War -- intensive frontier campaigns |
| 172 AD | **Victory over the Quadi** ("Thundering Legion" incident) |
| 175 AD | Avidius Cassius revolt (distracts from frontier war) |
| 176 AD | Triumph in Rome for Danubian victories |
| **178-180 AD** | **Second Marcomannic War** -- renewed campaigns |
| March 17, 180 AD | Marcus dies at Vindobona during the campaigns |
| 180 AD | Commodus negotiates peace and returns to Rome |

### Significance

- The Marcomannic Wars marked the **beginning of the Roman Empire's long struggle against Germanic incursions**
- They foreshadowed the later crises that would eventually lead to the fall of the Western Roman Empire
- The campaigns highlighted the strategic importance of the **Danube** as Rome's northern defensive line (*limes*)
- Marcus spent the last 14 years of his life largely on campaign, writing *Meditations* in military tents

> Source: Cassius Dio (Book 71-72), *Historia Augusta*, Column of Marcus Aurelius. **High credibility**.

## 8. Avidius Cassius Revolt (175 AD)

### Background

**Gaius Avidius Cassius** was a Roman general and governor of Syria who had served with distinction under Marcus Aurelius. He was of Roman senatorial family and had gained military prestige from the Parthian War (160s AD), particularly for his role in the sack of Ctesiphon in 165 AD.

### The Revolt

- **Date:** July/August 175 AD
- **Cause:** Cassius declared himself emperor, reportedly after receiving **false reports that Marcus Aurelius had died** from illness
- **Territory:** Cassius controlled **Syria, Egypt, and parts of the eastern provinces**
- **Duration:** Approximately **3 months and 6 days**

### Faustina's Alleged Involvement

Some sources (notably the *Historia Augusta*) suggest that **Faustina**, Marcus's wife, may have encouraged the revolt out of fear for her family's future if Marcus died. This is debated by historians and may be a later fabrication.

### Resolution

- Cassius was **killed by his own centurions** before Marcus needed to confront him militarily
- Marcus reportedly said he wished Cassius had come to him in person to discuss any grievances

### Marcus's Clemency

Marcus's response to the revolt was remarkable and consistent with his Stoic philosophy:

- He **forgave Cassius's family and supporters**
- He reportedly **burned Cassius's correspondence** to avoid punishing those who had written favorably to the usurper
- He traveled east to restore order in the provinces

### Aftermath

- **Faustina died** during the eastern journey (winter 175/176 AD), possibly in Cappadocia
- The revolt was largely **inconsequential** for Marcus's reign overall
- It demonstrated both the fragility of Roman power and Marcus's commitment to Stoic principles of justice and clemency

> Source: Cassius Dio (Book 71-72), *Historia Augusta* (Vita Marci, Vita Avidii Cassii). **High credibility** -- the revolt is well-attested; details about Faustina's involvement are debated.

## 9. Family Life and Personal Details

### Wife: Faustina the Younger

- **Full name:** Annia Galeria Faustina Minor
- **Born:** ~125-130 AD
- **Father:** Emperor Antoninus Pius
- **Mother:** Faustina the Elder
- **Marriage:** ~145 AD
- **Title:** Augusta (Empress)
- **Known for:** Loyalty, traveling with Marcus on military campaigns
- **Death:** Winter 175/176 AD, possibly in Cappadocia
- **Deified** by the Roman Senate after her death
- **Accusations:** Ancient sources (notably Cassius Dio) accused her of infidelity, though Marcus publicly defended her

### Children

Marcus and Faustina had **at least 13-14 children**, though many died in infancy or childhood. This was a source of great personal grief for Marcus.

### Personal Character

- Known for his **devotion to duty** and philosophy rather than luxury
- Struggled with **health problems** throughout his life (possibly stomach ulcers or other chronic conditions)
- Often described his reign as one of **exhaustion and duty** rather than pleasure
- Did **not remarry** after Faustina's death
- Was deeply affected by the deaths of many of his children, which influenced his Stoic reflections on mortality

> Source: *Historia Augusta*, Cassius Dio, *Meditations*. **High credibility** -- well-documented, though some details (especially about children) are uncertain.

## 10. The Succession Question: Commodus

### The Decision (177 AD)

In 177 AD, Marcus Aurelius elevated his biological son **Commodus** (born 161 AD, age ~16) to **co-emperor** (Augustus). This was a momentous decision:

- Commodus was the first emperor **"born to the purple"** (born during a father's reign)
- This broke the long-standing tradition of **adoptive succession** that had characterized the era of the Five Good Emperors
- Marcus co-ruled with Commodus until Marcus's death in 180 AD

### Why Marcus Made This Decision

Historians debate Marcus's motivations:

| Theory | Arguments |
|--------|-----------|
| **Love for his son** | Marcus may have wanted to secure a peaceful succession for his only surviving son |
| **Political pragmatism** | Naming a clear heir avoided civil war; the alternative could have been chaos |
| **Sense of duty** | Marcus may have felt obligated to establish succession stability |
| **Limited alternatives** | Marcus had few other viable candidates; adoption might not have been accepted |
| **Personal weakness** | Some historians argue Marcus was too attached to his son to make the rational choice |

### Consequences

1. **End of the adoptive system:** The merit-based selection of capable successors was abandoned
2. **Commodus's disastrous reign:** Commodus proved to be one of Rome's most tyrannical and incompetent emperors, obsessed with gladiatorial combat and megalomania
3. **Decline of the Roman Empire:** Gibbon famously marked the death of Marcus Aurelius as the beginning of Rome's decline
4. **Political instability:** Commodus was assassinated in 192 AD, leading to the chaotic **Year of the Five Emperors** (193 AD) and civil war

### The Debate

The question of whether Marcus had a better alternative (or whether he even wanted Commodus to succeed him but felt trapped by circumstance) remains a popular topic of historical debate. Some scholars argue that Marcus was simply being practical -- the alternative to hereditary succession was often civil war.

> Source: Cassius Dio, *Historia Augusta*, Gibbon (*Decline and Fall*). **High credibility** -- the decision and its consequences are well-documented; motivations are debated.

## 11. Manuscript and Textual History

### Original Composition

- **Title:** Τὰ εἰς ἑαυτόν (*Ta eis heauton*) -- "Things to Himself"
- **Language:** Koine Greek
- **Writing period:** ~170-180 AD
- **Intent:** Private philosophical journal, never intended for publication
- **Format:** 12 books (likely 12 physical notebooks or codices)

### Survival and Transmission

- The work's survival is remarkable given its private nature
- References by later authors (e.g., **Themistius** in the 4th century) suggest it circulated to some degree in antiquity
- There is essentially **one surviving primary manuscript tradition**
- The most important early source is the **Codex Vaticanus Graecus 1950**, a 14th-century copy housed in the Vatican Library
